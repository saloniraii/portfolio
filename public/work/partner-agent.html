Designing a headless partner agent

Partner Cloud is Salesforce's platform for managing resellers, distributors, and channel partners. I led the design of a conversation-native agent that meets partners where they already work — Slack, WhatsApp, SMS — instead of asking them to log into a portal they'd already abandoned.

Why isn't the portal working?

In collaboration with our Research team, we conducted a deep research into this problem. The core of the report can be boiled down into these three points.

Insight 01

The portal assumed captive attention

81% of the customer base (Scale-up + Micro-SaaS) had no dedicated channel managers to drive adoption. For them, the portal was a sunk cost.

Insight 02

Partners already had a workspace

WhatsApp, Slack, SMS. That's where deals moved and questions got asked. The portal was a side quest.

Insight 03

The jobs were conversational

Check pricing. Register a deal. Ask about MDF. These aren't GUI workflows — they're questions. The exact shape of a conversation.

The pivot

The existing roadmap was portal improvements. But the research made it clear — improving the portal wouldn't fix the problem, because the problem was the portal.

Partners weren't going to log in more. They needed the work to come to them. That meant proposing a fundamentally different direction to leadership — one that required new infrastructure, new channels, and a new mental model for the product.

Designing for conversation

The interface went through three fundamental shifts

Iteration 01

Started with forms in chat

The first instinct was to replicate portal forms in conversation — structured prompts, one field at a time, strict sequence. It worked technically, but felt like talking to a phone tree. Partners abandoned mid-flow at the same rate as the portal.

Iteration 02

Mapped how partners actually talk

We recorded real partner conversations over Slack and WhatsApp — messy, multi-intent, constantly interrupted. "Register a deal for Meridian, also what's my quota?" Nobody speaks in form fields. The patterns were nothing like structured flows.

Iteration 03

Conversation as the interface

The final model: the agent parses intent (not fields), confirms by summarizing back (not re-asking), and remembers context across interruptions. Every pattern went through 3+ rounds of script-testing with real partner managers before engineering saw it.

The Experience

We couldn't build every journey

So we ranked them on two axes: how often partners hit them, and how badly the portal handled them.

Deal registration and lead status checks sat in the worst quadrant of both — they drove the majority of partner escalations and were the biggest contributors to the 8-day case resolution cycle. Those became release one.

Just as deliberately, we deferred anything that needed rich UI to work well — dashboards, document review, multi-record comparison. Forcing those into a text thread would have proven the skeptics right. The scope wasn't just what conversation could do; it was what conversation could do better than the portal.

Relationship building

but it's not always the happy path, is it?

A GUI has guardrails built into its structure — disabled buttons, required fields, validation tooltips. In conversation, there are no guardrails. The user can say anything, in any order, at any time. Designing for the happy path is the easy part; the real work is deciding what happens when people go off-script.
