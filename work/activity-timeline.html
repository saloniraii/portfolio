The provocation

The timeline was designed for a world that no longer exists.

Activity timelines are the backbone of every CRM — Sales, Service, Marketing, Health, Financial Services. The one component that appears everywhere. And it was still a passive, chronological log built for human-only workflows.

22

activity types across Salesforce clouds. Zero intelligence applied to any of them.

Agents were already qualifying leads, triaging cases, sending follow-ups, updating records. But the timeline couldn't distinguish who did the work, why an agent acted, or what still needed human attention. We didn't wait for a brief. We started designing before anyone asked.

No attribution

Impossible to distinguish agent-performed activities from human ones. As agents generate more entries, users lose trust.

Manual bottleneck

A rep managing 50 accounts can't parse a timeline where agents logged 200 actions overnight. No filtering. No prioritization.

Linear in a parallel world

Sequential review of activities in a world where 3 agents collaborate on a single case simultaneously. A 2005 interface for 2026 workflows.

The design

From passive log to active orchestration layer

We redesigned the timeline from the ground up around three pillars — transforming it from an event log into an intelligent surface that reflects human-AI collaboration as a first-class concept.

All Engagements

A unified, queryable surface replacing the flat chronological feed. Natural language input lets users ask analytical questions — not just filter. Intelligent tabs (Up Next, Needs Attention, Completed) surface what matters.

Saved Insights

A persistent layer for intelligence worth returning to. When the timeline surfaces a pattern — sentiment shifts, risk signals — users save it. Insights accumulate as a curated knowledge base, shareable via Slack.

Agentic Attribution

Every activity visually distinguishes human from agent authorship. Agent activities surface rationale — why it acted — and recommend human action where needed. Filter the entire timeline by author type.

Key decisions

Four architectural departures from every CRM timeline on the market

Decision 01

Surface the "why," not just the "what"

Traditional timelines say "Email sent to Olivia." Ours says "Olivia's email signals interest but raises procurement concerns — potential Q4 delay." The timeline becomes a strategic advisor.

Decision 02

Show related, not just sequential

Toggle "Show related activities" breaks free from chronological order. The timeline dynamically reorganizes to show the constellation of activities behind an insight, regardless of when they occurred.

Decision 03

Multi-owner awareness

Enterprise records have multiple owners. Avatar piles and prompt attribution show who initiated each insight or action. The timeline becomes a shared workspace, not a personal feed.

Decision 04

Cross-cloud unification

Visual signifiers communicate connections between Marketing, Service, and Sales activities on a single record. One coherent feed with clear provenance — eliminating siloed views.

What made this patentable

These weren't incremental improvements. They were architectural innovations.

As we designed, we realized we were solving problems no one in the market had addressed. The ideas weren't just interesting — they were defensible.

Agent-attributed timelines

No CRM distinguishes agent work from human work in the timeline itself. Microsoft adds AI summaries but not attribution.

Natural language querying

No CRM lets you ask complex analytical questions of your timeline: "Average sentiment from emails this week?"

Actionable auto-summaries

No CRM auto-generates configurable, domain-specific summary cards with sentiment analysis on page load.
