Albus

I designed and led an AI knowledge bot from zero to 3,000+ organizations, 18,000+ users, and acquisition — before Slack, Microsoft, or anyone else had AI in workplace chat.

Context: A Startup Where Designers Own Everything

Springworks was a 200-person HR-tech company with a 4-person design team. There were no hand-offs. No "throw it over the wall." If you were the designer on a product, you were also part PM, part marketer, part support agent, and part growth strategist. Ownership wasn't a buzzword — it was the operating model.

I joined as a Junior Product Designer in 2022 and was promoted to Product Designer within a year. I worked across two products simultaneously:

  • EngageWith — The legacy employee recognition and rewards platform. High-revenue, established customer base. I designed the employee awards feature and worked on retention-focused enhancements.
  • Albus — A brand-new AI product. Zero users. Zero revenue. Built from scratch. This is the one I'm going to tell you about.

The Product: AI in Slack, Before Anyone Else

It's easy to forget now, but in late 2022, there was no AI in workplace chat. Slack didn't have Slack AI. Microsoft didn't have Copilot in Teams. The concept of asking a bot a question about your company's internal docs — and getting an accurate, sourced answer back in seconds — didn't exist as a product category.

We built it anyway.

Albus in action — a Slack conversation showing a user asking "What's our refund policy?" and Albus responding with the answer, source attribution, and a confidence indicator

Albus was a GPT-powered knowledge bot that lived inside Slack (and later Microsoft Teams). You'd connect it to your company's internal docs — Google Drive, Notion, Confluence, whatever — and it would index everything. Then any employee could ask a question in natural language, directly in Slack, and get an instant answer pulled from company knowledge with source citations.

The value proposition was simple: stop interrupting colleagues. Stop searching through 47 Google Docs. Just ask Albus.

We launched the MVP ChatGPT integration inside Slack and it grew to 3,000+ organizations and 18,000+ users in 8 weeks. The onboarding funnel hit 79% activation and 1% free-to-paid conversion. The product was later acquired in late 2024. In between, we hit Product Hunt's front page four times, got Microsoft 365 certified, achieved ISO 27001 compliance, and built a product that thousands of companies relied on daily.

Evolution: From Chat Toy to Enterprise Platform

The MVP proved demand. But a ChatGPT wrapper in Slack wasn't a defensible product. The real work was what came next — a series of product bets that transformed Albus from novelty into acquisition target. I designed the experiences for each of these:

V2: Company Knowledge Integration

I designed the v2 experience that absorbed information from company documents (Google Drive, Notion, Confluence) and resolved employee queries using GPT-4. This wasn't a feature — it was a product category pivot. We went from "ChatGPT in Slack" to "your company's AI brain." The result: average revenue per subscriber increased 15x.

Workplace Integration Strategy

The team identified a workplace integration strategy to increase adoption and distribution. My role: designing the touchpoints where Albus embedded into natural workflows rather than requiring top-down admin adoption. Adoption of our moat feature grew 4x within 6 months.

Internal Helpdesk: The Mid-Market Play

I designed the internal employee helpdesk experience that automated IT, HR, and manager escalations with AI. This wasn't just a feature — it was a market expansion move. The helpdesk unlocked mid-market companies who needed more than a knowledge bot; they needed a support system. Minimum annual contract values increased 6.3x.

The reliability problem. The team developed a multi-level search mechanism to reduce AI hallucination and search failures, improving reliability to answer questions correctly from 50% to 70%. I designed the UX patterns around uncertainty — graceful fallbacks, confidence signaling, and escalation flows — that made users trust the system even when it couldn't answer.

Retention & Expansion

I designed the automatic AI trigger system — the experience layer that drove adoption across internal teams and maintained retention. Later, agentic frameworks broke Albus into new problem statements for engineering, product, and customer-facing teams. Penetration within clients grew from 30% to 70% in US and European markets. Albus went from "the HR team's bot" to "the entire company's AI layer."

The Design Challenges

Designing a Bot That Feels Trustworthy

In 2022, people didn't trust AI answers. (Many still don't.) The core design challenge wasn't making Albus functional — it was making it credible. Every response needed to earn trust in the 3 seconds before a user decided whether to believe it or Google it themselves.

I designed the response pattern around three trust signals:

  • Source attribution — Every answer cited the specific document it came from, with a link to verify
  • Contextual follow-ups — If the bot sensed ambiguity, it asked a clarifying question rather than guessing — showing it "knew what it didn't know"
  • Graceful uncertainty — When Albus couldn't find an answer, it said so clearly and suggested who might know — rather than hallucinating

Making Setup Stupidly Simple

Our target market was lean teams (HR managers, ops leads, founders) who didn't have time to configure complex tools. The onboarding had to be near-zero friction:

  1. Install from Slack App Directory (1 click)
  2. Connect your Google Drive / Notion (OAuth, 2 clicks)
  3. Ask your first question (immediately)

No training. No configuration wizard. No "wait 24 hours for indexing." I designed the experience so that value was delivered in the first 60 seconds.

The onboarding flow — 3 steps from install to first answer. Show the simplicity: Slack install → connect Drive → ask a question

Designing for a Channel, Not a Screen

Slack isn't a canvas. You can't control layout. You can't use custom fonts. You're working within Block Kit's constraints — buttons, dropdowns, markdown text, and that's about it. Every design decision was about information hierarchy within a chat message.

I developed patterns for:

  • Long answers that needed to be scannable (progressive disclosure via "Show more")
  • Multi-source answers where the bot synthesized across documents
  • Ephemeral responses (only visible to the asker) vs. channel-visible responses
  • Action buttons for feedback ("Was this helpful?"), follow-up queries, and escalation to a human

Dashboard for Non-Technical Admins

The admin dashboard had to serve HR managers, not engineers. I designed it to answer three questions immediately on login:

  1. "Is it working?" — Activity stats, recent questions, answer accuracy
  2. "What are people asking?" — Query patterns, trending topics, unanswered questions
  3. "What should I do?" — Suggestions to improve coverage (upload more docs, clarify ambiguous content)

What This Taught Me

Working at Springworks was the opposite of working at Salesforce. No dedicated PM writing specs. No research team handing over findings. No design system to lean on. No 8-person engineering squad waiting for my Figma files. It was: figure it out, ship it, learn, repeat.

Three lessons I carry from this experience:

Ownership beats specialization. When you own the entire experience — from onboarding email to support ticket to dashboard redesign — you develop an instinct for what matters that pure "product design" work can't give you. You feel the pain of a confusing feature in the support queue the next day. That feedback loop is irreplaceable.

Speed is a design decision. At a startup, the fastest design that works is better than the perfect design that ships late. I learned to identify the 20% of craft that delivers 80% of the impact — and to ship the rest as iteration.

Being early to a market is its own kind of design challenge. When you're building in a category that doesn't exist yet, there are no patterns to follow. No competitors to study. No user expectations to meet. You're not optimizing — you're inventing. The Albus response format, the trust signals, the onboarding flow — none of these had precedent. I had to design them from first principles.
