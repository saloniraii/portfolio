Headless Partner Agent

How a design-led vision pivoted an entire product strategy and shipped as Salesforce's first conversation-native channel management tool

The Setup

Here's a number that should make any product team uncomfortable: 40 million partner users, and most of them never log in.

Partner Cloud is Salesforce's platform for managing indirect sales ecosystems—the resellers, distributors, and affiliate partners who collectively drive 75% of enterprise B2B revenue. We serve 8,000 customers who use our tools to recruit, onboard, enable, and compensate their partner networks. On paper, it's a massive, high-value product. In practice, we had an engagement crisis that no amount of portal polish could fix.

Opening stat — a bold data visualization showing 40M partner users, 4M monthly active, and the engagement gap. Hero image.

The symptoms were everywhere. Support cases took an average of 8 days to resolve because information was scattered across systems. 11% of cases ended with a channel manager simply sending the partner a link to a help article they could have found themselves—if only they'd logged in. Channel leaders at our customer organizations were openly questioning the ROI of their partner programs, despite those same programs generating the majority of their revenue.

When I joined this problem space, the team's strategy was focused on incremental improvements: better navigation, streamlined admin setup, AI-powered suggestions within the portal. Good work, but it assumed the portal was the right surface. I wasn't convinced it was.

The Insight That Changed Everything

I spent the first phase immersed in research—customer interviews, behavioral analysis, competitive landscape review, and deep collaboration with Salesforce's R&I team who had conducted a PRM Opportunity Assessment and a Seller Survey.

Research synthesis wall or insight map — showing the journey from raw data to the three core insights below

One data point from the R&I studies crystallized the opportunity: when Salesforce surveyed both its own customers and non-Salesforce users about what they most needed from an AI agent, partner support ranked #2 across all respondents. The demand was there. The delivery mechanism was wrong.

I mapped the actual daily workflow of partner representatives—not the idealized journey we'd designed for, but the real one. And the real one looked like this: a partner rep named "Russel" (our composite persona) starts his morning checking WhatsApp messages from customers. He drives to a client site. During the meeting, the client asks about pricing for a product he sells on behalf of his vendor. Russel needs the answer now—not after he drives back to his office, opens a laptop, logs into a portal he barely remembers the URL for, navigates to the pricing section, and searches for the right SKU.

Russel's vendor has 40 million partners like him. Each channel manager at the vendor engages with 500 of these partners every month. The math doesn't work through a portal. It never did.

Three insights emerged from this research:

The portal assumed captive attention. But partners aren't employees. They sell for multiple vendors simultaneously. They have no loyalty to any single portal. Our market analysis showed that 81% of our customer base (the Scale-up and Micro-SaaS segments, representing 36% and 45% respectively) lacked dedicated channel account managers who could drive portal adoption through relationship pressure. For these customers, the portal was a sunk cost.

Partners already had a primary workspace—messaging. WhatsApp, Slack, SMS. That's where deals moved. That's where questions got asked. That's where decisions happened. The portal was a side quest.

The jobs-to-be-done were conversational by nature. Check pricing. Register a deal. Ask about MDF balance. Get a case update. These aren't complex GUI workflows. They're questions and requests—the exact shape of a conversation.

The Vision: Meet Partners Where They Work

I proposed something that felt obvious once stated but radical in context: stop trying to get partners to come to us. Go to them.

Instead of improving the existing portal-based Partner Success Agent, I designed a headless, omnichannel partner agent—one that could operate natively across WhatsApp, Slack, and SMS, bringing the full power of Salesforce's partner management capabilities into a conversation thread.

Core concept diagram — a single agent identity deployed across multiple messaging surfaces (WhatsApp, Slack, SMS), all connecting back to the Salesforce CRM with Agentforce reasoning engine

"Headless" meant no GUI dependency. "Omnichannel" meant the same agent, same context, same capabilities—regardless of which messaging platform the partner preferred. The agent would be context-aware (understanding partner tier, assigned products, active deals) and surface-aware (adapting its response format to the constraints of each channel).

This wasn't a feature request or an incremental enhancement. It was a new product thesis. And to make it real, I had to design something no one at Salesforce had done before for partners: a complete conversation-first experience that could replace a web application.

Conversation Design: Writing the Product, Not Just Drawing It

This is where the work got deep. Designing a headless agent isn't wireframing screens—it's authoring dialogue. Every interaction pattern, every error state, every edge case exists as a conversation turn. I was simultaneously the interaction designer, the content strategist, and the information architect—except my medium was language, not layout.

Full conversation flow diagram for the Deal Registration topic — branching tree with decision nodes, data collection steps, validation checks, error recovery loops, and final record creation

I designed the complete conversational UX for six core agent topics:

Deal Registration

The most complex flow. On the portal, deal registration is a 15+ field form with picklists, date pickers, currency inputs, and file uploads. I had to translate this entire workflow into natural dialogue. The agent asks: "Who's the customer?" → "What product are they interested in?" → "When do you expect to close?" Each response gets intelligently mapped to the correct Salesforce object field. The partner never sees a form. They just have a conversation, and a fully valid Deal Registration record appears in their vendor's CRM.

MDF (Market Development Funds) Enquiries

Partners constantly need to know their fund balance—but a raw number is useless without context. I designed the agent's response pattern to always provide a contextual summary: total allocation, committed funds (approved but unpaid), available balance, and a proactive warning if any portion expires within 30 days. This replaced a portal page that required 4 clicks to reach and 3 different views to piece together.

Authentication

The hardest conversation design challenge. Partners on WhatsApp aren't logged into Salesforce. They need to prove their identity before accessing sensitive data—but the authentication flow can't break the conversational thread or feel like a security obstacle course. I designed a mid-conversation 2FA flow: the agent sends a verification code to the partner's registered email, the partner replies with the code in-chat, and authenticated capabilities unlock seamlessly. No redirect. No new window. No broken context.

Error Recovery & Graceful Degradation

Messaging platforms don't have form validation. If a partner types "next Tuesday" when the agent needs a date, or "$50K-ish" when it needs a precise currency value, the conversation can't crash. I designed re-prompt patterns that feel natural rather than robotic:

"Got it—around $50K. To submit this request, I'll need the exact amount. What should I put down?"
Side-by-side comparison — the Deal Registration experience as a portal form (15 fields, dropdowns, date pickers) vs. the same task as a 6-message WhatsApp conversation

Designing Within Brutal Constraints

WhatsApp has no interactive buttons in most regions. No carousels. No tables. No rich text formatting beyond bold and italic. Slack is more flexible but still limited compared to a web UI. Every design decision had to survive the worst-case surface.

I developed patterns that became the team's conversation design system:

  • Chunked responses — Long information broken into 2-3 messages rather than one wall of text
  • Numbered options — "Reply 1, 2, or 3" as a substitute for buttons
  • Narrative data presentation — Instead of a pricing table: "The SmartSolar Pro is $4,200/unit for orders under 50. For 50+, your partner tier gives you $3,780/unit—that's a 10% discount. Want me to generate a quote?"
  • Progressive skill unlocking — Unauthenticated partners get FAQ access only. The moment they ask something that requires data access, the agent naturally triggers the verification flow before continuing.

The Steel-Thread That Sold the Vision

A headless agent is abstract. Executives don't fund abstractions. So I crafted an end-to-end narrative demo—a steel-thread story that walked stakeholders through a single partner's day, showing how every interaction that previously required portal access could now happen in the flow of work.

Steel-thread storyboard — key frames from the Figma prototype showing Russel receiving a lead on WhatsApp, authenticating, registering a deal, checking pricing mid-meeting, and receiving a generated quote PDF

The story followed Russel from Alpine Solar through six scenarios: partner onboarding, training enrollment, lead distribution, deal negotiation (including a discount request while at the customer's office), MDF campaign initiation, and ecosystem performance review for his channel manager. Each scenario mapped to a real customer pain point from our research.

I built this as an interactive Figma prototype and a working Heroku mock that simulated the conversational flow. This became the centerpiece of our DVS (Design Vision Sprint) presentation to leadership in July 2025.

Securing the Bet

The vision work wouldn't matter if it stayed in Figma. I co-authored the confidential strategy proposal with my PM, framing the investment through three lenses:

Market positioning: No competitor offered headless, conversation-native partner management. This would make Salesforce the first.

Revenue model: I mapped conversation patterns to Agentforce Flex Credits—every agent interaction consumes credits. A simple FAQ costs 20 credits. A deal registration flow costs 40. A full guided onboarding journey costs 120. Multiply by 4M monthly active partners, and the headless agent becomes a significant Agentforce revenue driver.

De-risked execution: I proposed a phased approach—262 release for foundation (WhatsApp + Slack, 5 topics), 264 for skill expansion (MDF, loyalty, JBP summaries), 268 for channel expansion (SMS, iMessage). Each phase delivered standalone value.

Phased roadmap — three columns showing 262/264/268 releases with expanding channel support and topic coverage. Team allocation: 8 engineers, 1 release cycle per phase.

The proposal secured an 8-person engineering team, VP-level sponsorship, review by two Principal Architects, and pilot commitments from Avalara (~$250K ACV), AWS, and CrowdStrike.

Shipping: Where Vision Meets Sprint Boards

Execution required designing not just the partner-facing experience but also the admin setup experience. The original portal-based agent required 34 manual configuration clicks to set up routing, messaging channels, and agent queues. For a headless agent that needed to work across multiple surfaces, this complexity would have killed adoption before it started.

I collaborated with the Salesforce Go (guided setup) team to design an automated configuration experience. We got routing setup down to 1 click—a 97% reduction. The full Partner Agent deployment was reduced by 60-70% in admin effort compared to manual configuration.

Before/after setup flow — 34-step manual configuration collapsed to 1-click automation, with the Go interface showing the Partner Agent setup card

I also worked with engineering to resolve a critical constraint: the platform's ASA-type agent security mandate blocked the product pricing topic from functioning in release 258. I helped design workarounds that met security requirements while preserving the conversational pricing experience—a solution that shipped in the 258 patch release in time for Dreamforce.

Impact

The Headless Partner Agent shipped in the 262 release with WhatsApp and Slack support. But the impact extended far beyond a single feature launch:

  • Product strategy pivot. The vision I proposed became Partner Cloud's defining narrative. We went from "portal improvement" to "conversation-native channel management platform." The entire 264 and 268 roadmaps were structured around this thesis.
  • Company-wide influence. Other Salesforce clouds began exploring similar "meet users in their channel" patterns for employee-facing and customer-facing agents. The headless architecture became a reference model.
  • Customer validation. Three enterprise pilots (Avalara, AWS, CrowdStrike) committed before the product shipped—based on the design vision and interactive prototype alone.
  • Revenue framing adopted. The Flex Credits consumption model I mapped became part of the official "Agentforce for Partners: Capability Deepdive and Implementation Guide" used by sales teams.
  • Flagship SKU differentiator. The headless agent became the headline capability for the Partner Ecosystem Management (PEM) add-on, positioned as what makes PEM worth the investment over base PRM.

Reflection

The biggest lesson from this project: in 0-1 work, the designer's job isn't just to envision the product—it's to make the vision legible to everyone who needs to believe in it. I designed the conversations that partners would have with the agent. But I also designed the narrative that leadership needed to hear, the phased roadmap that de-risked the bet, and the financial model that made the opportunity undeniable.

Conversation design was central to this project in a way that traditional product design case studies rarely capture. There were no hero screens. No dramatic before/after UI comparisons. The product is the dialogue. And designing dialogue at enterprise scale—across constrained surfaces, with authentication requirements, error recovery needs, and platform limitations at every turn—is some of the most technically demanding design work I've done.

The Headless Partner Agent is still expanding. The 264 release adds MDF and Joint Business Plan skills. 268 will bring SMS and iMessage. But the strategic foundation—the argument that partners should be met where they work, not where we build—that shipped in the first vision presentation, months before a single line of code was written. That's the work I'm most proud of.
